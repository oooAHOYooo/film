Right now you have the bones of one, which is genuinely more than most scripts have.

Here's a deep pull across the classics — organized by what specific mechanism you're stealing and how it maps to your script.
From Signs
The "everything was pointing somewhere" reveal. In Signs every character quirk is secretly a plot device. Audit every recurring detail in your script and ask whether it pays off. Dominic's inhaler never pays off. Give it a moment. Maybe the frequency the creature responds to is the same pitch as the inhaler's spray and Dominic accidentally soothes the creature with it in a funny scene early, and then deliberately uses it in the marsh when the equipment fails. Now it's a Chekhov's inhaler.
The "no coincidences" speech. Signs has Mel Gibson's monologue about people who see signs and people who don't. Your script needs a version of that — and Dominic should give it. He's already circling it with his "boom, gone" speech on the marsh walk. Expand that. Let him articulate a worldview. Let Dallas push back. Let that tension between faith and empiricism run as a visible thread through the whole film rather than sitting quietly in the background.
The baby monitor scene. Signs uses cheap technology to make contact with something terrifying feel unbearably intimate. You have this opportunity with Dallas's recording equipment. The moment he plays back the tape and hears the creature's frequency for the first time should feel like the baby monitor scene — small, domestic, and world-expanding.
From E.T.
The secret in the house that the kid is hiding from the adult. Elliott hiding E.T. from his mother generates the entire middle act of that film. You have the creature hidden from Dominic and Janice but the stakes of discovery feel more logistical than emotional. Make it more personal — Dominic finding the creature shouldn't just be awkward, it should risk something real in his relationship with Makayla and Asher. They're orphans living in his house. His trust is not a small thing to lose.
The government as ambient threat rather than active villain. E.T. doesn't need a mustache-twirling villain because the threat is systemic — institutions, adults, the way the world handles things it doesn't understand. You have Pat Clendenen and his news vans. One more beat where Pat gets closer to the truth than he realizes — maybe his equipment accidentally picks up the frequency and he dismisses it as interference — would add this layer without needing a new character.
The creature getting sick in act two. E.T. nearly dying is the emotional gut-punch that makes the ending land. Your creature is injured throughout but never seems to be at genuine risk of dying until very late. Give it a crisis earlier — a moment where it goes completely still and the glow fades entirely and everyone in the room thinks it's gone. Let Makayla and Asher react to that loss. Then let it come back. Now the audience is emotionally bonded to it in a way they weren't before.
The bike ride equivalent. E.T. has one purely joyful sequence where the danger falls away and wonder takes over completely. Your script doesn't have this yet. It goes from investigation directly to threat. You need one scene — maybe Dallas and Makayla sitting with the creature at night when it's healthy and glowing and they're just watching it hum, and Asher is there tracing its pattern on his palm, and nobody says anything, and it's just beautiful — before everything goes wrong. That contrast is what makes the danger feel like loss rather than just action.
From Close Encounters of the Third Kind
The protagonist who becomes obsessed in ways that alienate everyone around him. Roy Neary building the Devil's Tower in his living room. Dallas is too contained. His obsession should have a cost. Maybe he misses something important — a commitment to Dominic, a moment with Makayla — because he's alone with his equipment at 3am. Let his grief and his fixation be slightly frightening to the people who are starting to care about him before it becomes heroic.
The shared vision across disconnected people. In Close Encounters multiple people across the world are receiving the same signal without knowing why. Mr. Mike has been writing the numbers for years. Asher has been tracing the patterns. Sierra had the field notes. The script gestures at this but doesn't make it feel cosmic enough. One scene where Dallas lays Sierra's notes next to Makayla's research next to Mr. Mike's scratchings and they all point to the same coordinates — that's your Close Encounters moment. It should feel like the universe has been trying to have this conversation for decades.
The communication breakthrough as the emotional climax. The five-note sequence in Close Encounters is the most purely joyful moment in Spielberg's filmography because it's humans and something unknown finding a shared language. Your equivalent is Dallas broadcasting at 115.3 and the creature mimicking it back. That scene needs to be bigger emotionally. Slower. Let Dallas not breathe. Let Makayla grab his arm without realizing she's doing it. Let Asher smile for the first time in the film. Make that moment the miracle it actually is before the Red-Eyes arrive and the action takes over.
From The Iron Giant
The adult who is changed by the creature rather than just helping it. Hogarth is wonderful but it's Dean McCoppin — the junkyard artist, the cynic — who is most transformed by the Giant. Dallas is already in this role but his transformation needs to be more visible. What does he believe at the end that he didn't believe at the start? It can't just be that he's less sad. It has to be something active — a new relationship to Sierra's memory, a new understanding of what she was chasing, a willingness to keep listening when before he was ready to stop.
The "I am not a gun" moment. The Giant choosing what it is rather than what it was built to be. Your creatures have a version of this — the golden ones are gentle despite clearly having enormous power. But you could make this more explicit and more poignant. A moment where a Red-Eye has the creature cornered and the Elder could destroy everything but chooses the frequency of protection over the frequency of violence. That choice, witnessed by Dallas, could be the thing that unlocks his grief. He watches a creature choose gentleness when it had every reason not to and something in him breaks open.
The sacrifice that isn't permanent but feels like it is. The Giant's apparent death is one of the great gut-punches in family film history because the movie earns it completely before revealing the Giant is reassembling. Your creature returning to the marsh has this shape but it needs more grief in it. Dallas should believe for a moment — maybe several scenes — that the creature is gone and that the recording is all that's left. Then the final image of the hum in the creek hits differently because it's reunion rather than just conclusion.
From Jurassic Park
The expert who knows the science but underestimates the wonder. Alan Grant spends the first act being irritated by children and the second act learning that wonder and chaos are the same thing. Dallas is doing a version of this — the bioacoustician who has measured everything and felt nothing — but Grant's arc works because Spielberg puts him in physical situations that force emotional response. Dallas needs more of those. The marsh sequence does this but it comes very late. Earlier in the film Dallas should encounter something his equipment can't explain and have to just stand there and experience it without recording it.
The moment where the glass of water ripples. Pure cinema. A warning delivered through the wrong medium, noticed by the wrong person, almost too late. You have Sarah's tea rippling in her cup during the life group scene and it's wonderful. Make more of it. Let it be the first sign that something is wrong before the hum becomes audible. Let a child notice it before an adult does. That's always the Spielberg move.
The kitchen scene tension. The raptor kitchen sequence is the most purely constructed suspense scene in Spielberg's filmography — two children, enclosed space, predator that can't quite see them. Your life group scene with the creature in the basement is structurally identical and you're not fully exploiting it. The retired vet who offers to look at the cat is your raptor. She should get closer than anyone realizes. Makayla should have to do something genuinely risky to redirect her. That scene should be excruciating.
From Poltergeist
The house itself as a character. Poltergeist makes the suburban home feel alive and then makes it feel hostile and then makes it feel like a trap. Your script uses Dallas's house well but it could use it more expressively. The burn marks spreading up the siding, the lights dimming, the windows rattling — these are great details but they need earlier, subtler versions. A door that won't stay closed. A room that always feels colder than it should. Howie refusing to go into a specific corner. Build the house's awareness gradually so when it becomes overtly hostile in Act III the audience has been primed.
The family as the unit of survival. Poltergeist is ultimately about a marriage and a family holding together under impossible pressure. Your found family — Dallas, Makayla, Asher, Dominic — needs one scene of genuine fracture before the finale. Not just Dominic being upset about the cat lie. A real rupture. Makayla accusing Dallas of only caring about the science. Dallas saying something that reveals he's been using the investigation to avoid grieving. Dominic having to choose between protecting his niece and nephew and doing the sensible thing. That fracture makes the marsh sequence feel like genuine reunion rather than just teamwork.
From Midnight Special
The child who is a receiver for something adults can't access. Alton in Midnight Special picks up signals that are destroying his body because he was built to receive them. Asher is this character and you're not pushing it far enough. His trance episodes should be more frightening earlier. The pattern tracing should feel compulsive, almost painful. When he finally uses his ability deliberately in the marsh it should feel like he's risking something — like he's opening a channel that could consume him — and Makayla should know that and let him do it anyway because there's no other choice. That's the most devastating beat available to you in this script and it's currently just a cool moment.
The parent who doesn't understand but shows up anyway. Midnight Special's emotional core is a father protecting a child he can't fully comprehend. Dominic is this person for Makayla and Asher and the script knows it but underplays it. His decision to pick up that eighty pound speaker and wade into the marsh should be the culmination of something we've been watching build — his love for these kids overwhelming his fear and his pragmatism. Give him a moment just before that where he almost turns back. Let him choose them explicitly.
From A Quiet Place
The constraint as metaphor. A Quiet Place makes sound itself the enemy and that formal constraint becomes an expression of grief — a family that has learned to communicate without words because words are too dangerous, which is also what grief does to people. Your script has the inverse of this premise — sound is salvation, frequency is connection, listening is love — and that's genuinely beautiful. But you could make the metaphor more formal. What if Dallas has been unable to listen to anything since Sierra died? Not literally — he still does his work — but emotionally he's been broadcasting and never receiving. The creature teaches him to receive again. That's your thematic constraint and it should be visible from scene one.
The loss that the family doesn't talk about. In A Quiet Place the dead son is present in every scene as an absence. Sierra should be more like this. Not just objects on a shelf but a presence in Dallas's behavior — the way he sets two mugs out of habit, the way he starts to say something and stops, the way he angles his equipment the way she taught him. Small things that accumulate into an unbearable portrait of someone we never meet.
From Arrival
The scientific methodology as emotional journey. Louise Banks in Arrival uses linguistics the way a surgeon uses a scalpel — with total precision — and that professional control is what makes her emotional breakthrough so devastating when it comes. Dallas's bioacoustics should feel equally precise and equally limiting. His equipment is the thing keeping him at a safe distance from what he's actually experiencing. The moment he puts the equipment down and just listens — really listens, the way Sierra would have — should be the emotional climax of the film regardless of what's happening with the creatures.
The recording that means something different the second time you hear it. Arrival's entire structure is built on this. Your script has Sierra's cassette tape and the creature's hum and the final revelation that they match. That's an Arrival move. But Arrival works because the audience has been primed to reinterpret information. Plant something in Act I — a specific sound, a specific frequency, a specific pattern — that seems like texture and turns out to be the key. When Dallas plays the recording at the end and the audience recognizes it before he does, that's when the film becomes something they'll think about for days.
The One Idea That Ties All of Them Together
Every great family film in this register is about the same thing underneath the genre mechanics. It's about whether the world is meaningful or indifferent. Whether the things that happen to us — including the worst things — are random or whether they're part of a pattern too large to see from inside your own grief.
Signs answers that question with faith. Close Encounters answers it with wonder. The Iron Giant answers it with choice. Arrival answers it with love surviving even the knowledge of loss.
Your script is reaching for the Arrival answer. Dallas discovers that Sierra was right — the world is full of frequencies we can't hear, and she spent her life trying to hear them, and now something is trying to communicate on exactly the frequency of her voice. That's not random. That's the universe being generous in the only language a bioacoustician could receive.
Make sure every scene is quietly asking that question. And make sure the final image answers it in a way that makes the audience feel, not think.
That's your movie.
